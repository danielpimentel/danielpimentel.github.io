Subspaces lie at the heart of data analysis. As soon as we get our hands on some data, finding a subspace that explains it is one of the first things we try, for example, using Principal Component Analysis (PCA) or linear regression.

Many applications have missing data and gross errors. For example, in computer vision, occlusions produce missing data; in surveys and recommender systems, subjects do not know or do not want to provide all information.

The main focus of my research is learning subspaces from highly incomplete and corrupted data. This has numerous applications in networks, computer vision, drug-discovery, rigidity theory, recommender systems, surveys, and pretty much anywhere there are subspaces and incomplete or corrupted data.

My research interests include signal processing, machine learning, optimization, statistics, and algebraic geometry.

Subspaces & Missing Data

We often want to find a subspace that explains a dataset. But often data is missing, so we cannot use our usual tricks, like PCA. To build some intuition of the difficulties of this problem, suppose I give you one datapoint (no missing data), and ask you to find the 1-dimensional subspace S that explains it. Easy: it is the subspace going from the origin to the given point.




Now suppose I give you one datapoint without the z coordinate. Then we don't even know where the point is, and we can no longer find the subspace that explains it. There are infinitely many options!




If I give you an other incomplete point with the (y,z) coordinates, then you would be able to find S. This would not be the case if I gave you an other incomplete point without the z coordinate.

The question is: if I give you a collection of incomplete points, where do I need to observe them such that they uniquely define an r-dimensional subspace? Whence, how do I identify such a subspace? This has been the main focus of my work.

Collection of incomplete points are usually given in the form of a partially observed matrix. A matrix is rank-r iff all its columns lie in an r-dimensional subspace. Hence this problem is usually called Low-Rank Matrix Completion (LRMC). If we knew S we could infer the missing entries. Conversely, if we knew the missing entries, we could infer S. So this is like a chicken and egg problem.


Unions of Subspaces

Sometimes we have an incomplete data matrix whose columns lie in a union of subspaces. The goal is to infer the underlying subspaces and cluster the columns accordingly. This is often known as incomplete-data Subspace Clustering (iSC).



iSC is the generalization of LRMC to the case where columns lie in several subspaces, rather than just one. Similarly, iSC is the generalization of Subspace Clustering to the case where data is incomplete. The main question is: when can we hope to solve this problem, and how?


Gross Errors

One is often given a data matrix whose columns lie in a low-dimensional subspace (as in the left), but every column may have a few grossly corrupted entries (as in the right). This makes every point an outlier.




The goal is to identify the underlying subspace. This problem is often known as Robust PCA. If columns lie in a union of subspaces, rather than just one, the problem is called Robust Subspace Clustering.
